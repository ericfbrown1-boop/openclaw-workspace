# 2. OFFSHORE LLC JURISDICTIONS COMPARISON

## Overview of Offshore Yacht Ownership Structures

Offshore LLC structures for yacht ownership serve multiple purposes: asset protection, tax optimization, privacy, and operational flexibility. The choice of jurisdiction significantly impacts tax treatment, regulatory compliance, ongoing costs, and legal protections.

## Marshall Islands: Primary Recommendation

### Legal Framework
The Republic of the Marshall Islands (RMI) offers one of the world's most yacht-friendly LLC structures, modeled after Delaware law but with offshore advantages.

**Key Advantages:**
- **Zero Income Tax**: No corporate income tax on non-RMI source income
- **Privacy Protection**: Beneficial owners not publicly disclosed
- **U.S. Coast Guard Recognition**: RMI is a full U.S. Coast Guard recognized flag state
- **Check-the-Box Election**: Can elect U.S. tax treatment as disregarded entity or partnership
- **Low Annual Fees**: Approximately $700/year (compared to $2,259 for Cayman Islands)
- **Rapid Formation**: 24-48 hours with expedited service
- **No Audit Requirements**: For standard LLCs

**Operational Characteristics:**
- Accepts single-member or multi-member structures
- No residency requirements for members or managers
- Minimal reporting requirements
- English is official language; U.S. dollar is currency
- Common law legal system based on U.S. model

**Taxation for U.S. Owners:**
When a Marshall Islands LLC makes a "check-the-box" election to be treated as a disregarded entity (single-member) or partnership (multi-member) for U.S. tax purposes:
- LLC itself pays no U.S. federal income tax (pass-through entity)
- U.S. owner reports income/losses on personal return
- Eligible for Section 179, bonus depreciation, MACRS, Section 199A
- State tax treatment varies by owner's domicile and nexus

**Cost Structure:**
- Formation: $1,500-$2,500 (including registered agent)
- Annual Renewal: $700-$900
- Registered Agent: $500-$800/year
- Legal/Accounting: $3,000-$5,000 annually

**Best For:**
- U.S. residents seeking tax optimization through U.S. pass-through treatment
- Owners requiring U.S. Coast Guard documentation
- Operators needing flexibility for Caribbean/Bahamas charters
- Cost-conscious owners seeking offshore benefits at lower fees

### Cayman Islands LLC

**Legal Framework:**
The Cayman Islands Limited Liability Company (LLC) Act (Revised 2020) was deliberately modeled after Delaware LLC law, providing familiar structure with offshore advantages.

**Key Advantages:**
- **No Direct Taxation**: Zero corporate income tax, capital gains tax, or withholding tax
- **Maximum Privacy**: Beneficial ownership registry is confidential (not public)
- **Political Stability**: British Overseas Territory with stable government
- **Sophisticated Financial Infrastructure**: Well-established banking, legal, and accounting services
- **Asset Protection**: Strong creditor protection laws
- **International Recognition**: Globally recognized and respected jurisdiction

**Operational Characteristics:**
- Highly flexible operating agreements
- No annual meetings or resolutions required
- No audit or financial filing requirements for standard LLCs
- Manager-managed or member-managed structures
- English language and common law system

**Cost Structure:**
- Formation: $3,000-$5,000
- Annual Government Fee: $1,219 (fixed by law)
- Annual Registered Office: $1,040
- **Total Annual Renewal: $2,259** (significantly higher than Marshall Islands)
- Legal/Compliance: $4,000-$7,000 annually

**Tax Treatment for U.S. Owners:**
- Can elect "check-the-box" treatment for U.S. tax purposes
- Treated as disregarded entity or partnership
- Same U.S. tax deductions available as Marshall Islands
- Economic Substance requirements apply to certain activities

**Considerations:**
- Economic Substance Act requires demonstration of adequate substance in Cayman for certain activities
- OECD Common Reporting Standard (CRS) reporting obligations
- Higher ongoing costs than Marshall Islands (~3x annual fees)

**Best For:**
- High-net-worth individuals prioritizing privacy
- International operations requiring maximum global recognition
- Owners with existing Cayman banking/trust relationships
- Those seeking enhanced asset protection in sophisticated legal environment

## Delaware LLC: Domestic Alternative

### Legal Framework
Delaware is the preeminent U.S. domestic jurisdiction for LLC formation, with over 1 million entities registered.

**Key Advantages:**
- **Well-Established Law**: Over 200 years of corporate case law precedent
- **Court of Chancery**: Specialized business court with expert judges
- **Flexibility**: Highly flexible operating agreements and management structures
- **Privacy**: Members/managers not required in public filings
- **No State Income Tax**: For LLCs with no Delaware-source income and non-Delaware resident members
- **Creditor Protection**: Strong charging order protections
- **Ease of Compliance**: Straightforward annual reporting

**Operational Characteristics:**
- Formation in 24 hours or less
- Annual franchise tax: $300
- No minimum capital requirements
- Registered agent required (Delaware address)
- Operating agreement not required to be filed publicly

**Cost Structure:**
- Formation: $90 state filing fee + $500-$1,000 for registered agent/legal services
- Annual Franchise Tax: $300
- Registered Agent: $50-$300/year
- **Total Annual Cost: $350-$600**

**Tax Treatment:**
- Pass-through taxation (disregarded entity or partnership)
- Full access to U.S. federal tax benefits (Section 179, bonus depreciation, MACRS, 199A)
- State tax depends on owner's state of residence and vessel usage location
- No Delaware state income tax if no Delaware source income and non-resident members

**Compliance Requirements:**
- Annual report filing
- Maintain registered agent
- File tax returns in states with nexus
- Documentation for business purpose

**Best For:**
- U.S. residents not requiring offshore privacy
- Owners prioritizing ease of banking and financing
- Operators seeking maximum legal certainty under U.S. law
- Budget-conscious owners seeking lowest-cost professional structure

## Wyoming LLC: Low-Cost Domestic Option

### Legal Framework
Wyoming was the first U.S. state to enact LLC legislation (1977) and maintains highly favorable business laws.

**Key Advantages:**
- **No State Income Tax**: Wyoming has no corporate or personal income tax
- **Strong Privacy**: Beneficial ownership not disclosed publicly
- **Asset Protection**: Among strongest charging order protections in U.S.
- **Low Costs**: Minimal filing fees and annual requirements
- **No Annual Report Requirement**: Unlike most other states
- **Business-Friendly**: Pro-business legislation and regulatory environment

**Cost Structure:**
- Formation: $100 state filing + $500 registered agent/legal
- Annual License Tax: $60 + $2 per $1,000 of Wyoming-based assets (minimal for out-of-state yacht)
- Registered Agent: $50-$150/year
- **Total Annual Cost: $110-$210** (lowest cost option)

**Operational Characteristics:**
- Single or multi-member
- Manager-managed or member-managed
- No annual report filing required
- Must file biennial report (every 2 years)
- Minimal state compliance

**Tax Treatment:**
- Pass-through federal taxation
- No Wyoming state income tax
- Subject to state taxes where owner resides and/or vessel operates
- Full federal deductions available

**Best For:**
- Cost-sensitive owners seeking maximum privacy and protection domestically
- Those avoiding offshore structure complexity
- Owners with established Wyoming relationships
- Supplemental structure to offshore entity (Wyoming LLC as member of Marshall Islands LLC)

## Florida LLC: Home State Considerations

### Legal Framework
Florida offers a straightforward LLC structure, relevant given likely Florida operations and potential sales tax implications.

**Key Advantages:**
- **No State Income Tax**: Florida has no personal income tax; corporate income tax applies only to C-Corps
- **Coastal State Benefits**: Direct registration for Florida vessel operations
- **Sales Tax Exemption Path**: Commercial vessel exemption potential
- **Simplified Compliance**: Direct state relationship
- **Streamlined Banking**: Easier banking relationships for Florida-based LLC

**Cost Structure:**
- Formation: $125 state filing + $500 legal
- Annual Report: $138.75
- Registered Agent: $100-$250/year
- **Total Annual Cost: $238-$388**

**Critical Tax Consideration:**
If LLC has Florida nexus (office, employees, dock, etc.), there are Florida corporate tax implications:
- Florida Corporate Income Tax: 5.5% on federal taxable income
- For single-member LLC: Disregarded, reported on member's return
- **Key Issue**: Florida sales/use tax on vessel purchase

**Florida Sales Tax Implications:**
- **Standard Rate**: 6% sales tax on vessel purchases
- **Cap**: Maximum tax of $18,000 (effectively caps taxable amount at $300,000)
- **Commercial Exemption**: Vessels purchased solely for commercial charter may be exempt
- **Out-of-State Delivery**: Can avoid Florida sales tax if delivered out of state and meets conditions
- **Use Tax**: Applies if brought to Florida within 6 months of purchase

3. Keep vessel outside Florida for 6+ months
4. Document commercial charter purpose
5. Maintain charter management agreement
6. **Potential Sales Tax Savings: $18,000** (the capped amount would apply regardless)

**Best For:**
- Florida residents operating primarily in Florida waters
- Those prioritizing simplicity over offshore benefits

- Situations where Florida nexus is unavoidable

# 3. U.S. TAX RULES & COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK

## Overview of Tax Treatment for Charter Yacht Operations

A yacht operated as a charter business is treated as a trade or business under IRC Section 162, making it eligible for business expense deductions, depreciation, and various tax benefits. However, the IRS applies strict scrutiny to yacht charter activities due to the personal enjoyment component, requiring careful attention to multiple code sections and regulations.

## Section 183: The Hobby Loss Rule

### Legal Standard

Internal Revenue Code Section 183 ("Activities Not Engaged in for Profit") prevents taxpayers from deducting losses from activities that are primarily personal hobbies rather than genuine business ventures.

**Key Principle:** If an activity is not engaged in for profit, deductions are limited to gross income from that activity—no losses can be used to offset other income.

### The Nine-Factor Test

The IRS and courts apply nine factors to determine profit motive (Treasury Regulation 1.183-2):

#### 1. Manner in Which Activity is Carried On

**Business-Like Operations Indicators:**
- Professional charter management agreement with established company
- Separate business bank account and accounting records
- Business plan with revenue projections and operational procedures
- Marketing materials and website
- Professional crew uniforms and conduct
- Regular maintenance schedule
- Detailed booking and reservation systems

**Red Flags:**
- Informal, casual operations
- Commingled personal and business funds
- Lack of written agreements
- No advertising or marketing efforts
- Irregular or sporadic charter activity

**Action Items for $7M Yacht:**
- Engage professional charter management company (Fraser Yachts, Northrop & Johnson, etc.)
- Develop written business plan with 7-year projections
- Create dedicated LLC bank account
- Implement QuickBooks or similar accounting system
- Establish professional website and listing on charter platforms
- Maintain detailed logs: maintenance, charter bookings, expenses, hours

#### 2. Expertise of the Taxpayer or Advisors

**Positive Indicators:**
- Owner has relevant maritime business experience
- Charter management company with industry expertise
- Professional captain with USCG license
- Advisors include maritime attorney, CPA familiar with yacht taxation
- Owner invests time in learning charter business

**Eric Brown's Position:**
- CFO/COO experience demonstrates business acumen
- Engagement of specialized tax and legal advisors
- Professional charter management relationship
- Evidence of research and planning (this analysis)

**Documentation:**
- Retain correspondence with charter management advisors
- Document due diligence in selecting professionals
- Maintain records of industry research
- Attend industry events (Fort Lauderdale Boat Show, Miami Yacht Show)

#### 3. Time and Effort Expended

**Sufficient Involvement Indicators:**
- Regular review of charter bookings and financials
- Participation in marketing decisions
- Periodic vessel inspections
- Review of maintenance reports
- Management of professional team

**Note:** Don't need to be full-time; can delegate to professionals but must maintain oversight.

**Time Log Recommendations:**
- Track hours spent on charter business management
- Document meetings with charter manager, captain, accountant
- Log time reviewing financial reports
- Record vessel inspections and maintenance oversight
- Target: 100+ hours annually in management activities

#### 4. Expectation of Asset Appreciation

**Application to Yachts:**
Yachts are depreciating assets, which works against this factor. However:
- Some vessels maintain value well (Marlow, Azimut quality brands)
- Proper maintenance can minimize depreciation
- Charter income can exceed depreciation
- Business can be profitable even with asset depreciation

**Mitigation:**
- Focus on income generation, not capital appreciation
- Select quality vessels known for value retention
- Document market research on resale values
- Maintain vessel in exceptional condition
- Keep comprehensive maintenance records

#### 5. Success in Other Similar or Dissimilar Activities

**Eric Brown's Advantage:**
- Proven track record as CFO/COO of successful technology company
- Experience managing complex operations and finances
- Business success demonstrates capability for profitable ventures

**Documentation:**
- Professional biography highlighting business achievements
- Cohesity success and growth metrics
- Other successful business ventures
- Board positions or advisory roles

#### 6. History of Income or Losses

**Critical Timeline:**
The IRS presumes an activity is for-profit if it shows a profit in at least 3 of the last 5 years (IRC 183(d)).

**First Three Years are Critical:**
- Year 1 (2026): Likely loss due to depreciation, startup
- Year 2 (2027): Should approach break-even
- Year 3 (2028): Should show profit or near-profit
- Years 4-5: Consistent profitability expected

**Strategies:**
- Conservative first-year depreciation elections to show profit sooner
- Guaranteed minimum charter agreements
- Aggressive marketing in Years 1-2
- Consider Section 183(e) election (postpones determination for 5 years)

**Section 183(e) Election:**
File Form 5213 within 3 years of starting activity to postpone IRS determination until you have 5 years of results. This prevents immediate IRS challenges but flags the return.

#### 7. Amount of Occasional Profits

**Even if Losses Some Years:**
If profitable years show substantial profit relative to investments and losses, this supports profit motive.

**Charter Business Realities:**
- Year 1: Setup costs and limited charter season = loss
- Years 2-3: Breaking even as charter base builds
- Years 4+: Profitable operations

**Documentation:**
- Project cash flow showing profitability trajectory
- Compare to industry benchmarks
- Document how early losses are typical startup costs
- Show how operational changes improve profitability

#### 8. Financial Status of Taxpayer

**The "Can Afford Losses" Problem:**
High-income taxpayers (like Eric Brown) face skepticism—IRS may argue they can afford losses and are subsidizing a hobby.

**Counter-Arguments:**
- Wealthy individuals also engage in legitimate businesses
- Business decisions made independent of personal wealth
- Profit motive exists regardless of need for income
- Activity structured and operated professionally

**Key Strategy:**
Never rely on other income; demonstrate charter business stands on its own:
- Separate LLC with own financing
- Professional management paid from charter income
- Business decisions based on charter economics
- No personal funds subsidizing operations beyond initial capital

#### 9. Elements of Personal Pleasure or Recreation

**The Biggest Challenge for Yacht Charters:**
Yachts inherently involve personal enjoyment, which is the primary basis for IRS scrutiny.

**Mitigation Strategies:**
1. **Limit Personal Use:** Stay well below 14-day safe harbor
2. **Charge Fair Market Value:** Pay full charter rates for any personal use
3. **Document Business Purpose:** Every trip should have business purpose when possible
4. **Professional Separation:** Owner is clearly a client when using vessel, not operator
5. **Arms-Length Treatment:** Treat personal use like any other charter client

**Personal Use Tracking:**
- Maintain detailed calendar of all vessel usage
- Classify each day: Charter, Maintenance, Deadhead, Personal, Marketing
- For personal use: Written charter agreement with owner, payment at market rate
- Compare personal use to charter days (should be <10%)

### IRS Yacht Charter Examination Cases

#### Notable Case Law:

**Buehler v. Commissioner (T.C. Memo 2023-128):**
- Taxpayer placed luxury yacht in charter program
- Personal use exceeded business use
- No profit in any year
- Casual, hobby-like operations
- **Result:** Section 183 applied; deductions limited to income

**Key Lessons:**
- Charter days must exceed personal days
- Profit in some years essential
- Business-like operations non-negotiable

**Successful Charter Cases:**
- Engdahl v. Commissioner (1994): Yacht charter business respected where taxpayer showed business operations, profit motive, time spent
- Jackson v. Commissioner (1987): Charter yacht allowed business deductions despite personal use when proper segregation and pricing

## Section 162: Trade or Business Deductions

### Qualifying as Trade or Business

To deduct expenses under IRC Section 162, activity must constitute a "trade or business"—requiring:
1. Profit motive (see Section 183 above)
2. Regular and continuous activity
3. Primary purpose is income generation

**Application to Charter Yacht:**
- Charter management agreement = continuous activity
- Regular marketing and booking efforts
- Professional crew and operations
- Active management of asset

### Deductible Operating Expenses

Once qualified as trade or business, all ordinary and necessary expenses are deductible:

#### Direct Operating Expenses:
- **Crew Salaries:** Captain, deckhands, stewardess, engineer ($150,000-$400,000/year for $7M yacht)
- **Crew Benefits:** Health insurance, payroll taxes, 401(k) matching
- **Fuel:** $80,000-$150,000 annually depending on usage
- **Dockage/Marina Fees:** $50,000-$150,000 annually (varies by location)
- **Insurance:** $50,000-$100,000 for commercial charter coverage
- **Maintenance & Repairs:** $140,000-$350,000 annually (2-5% of vessel value)
- **Provisions:** Food, beverages, supplies for charters ($20,000-$40,000)
- **Licenses & Permits:** USCG documentation, business licenses, charter permits
- **Communications:** Satellite internet, phone systems ($10,000-$20,000)

#### Charter Management Fees:
- **Management Fee:** 20-35% of gross charter revenue
- **Marketing Fee:** Often included, or separate 5-10%
- **Booking Commission:** Sometimes separate 10-15%
- **Total Management Cost:** Typically 25-35% of gross revenue

- If personal use below 14 days and not charged: Allocate based on use percentage
- If personal use exceeds 14 days: See dwelling unit rental rules below

## Section 280A: Dwelling Unit Rental Rules

### When Section 280A Applies

If the yacht qualifies as a "dwelling unit" (has sleeping, cooking, toilet facilities—which all yachts do), IRC Section 280A can limit deductions if there is personal use.

**Threshold:** Section 280A applies if personal use exceeds the greater of:
- 14 days per year, OR
- 10% of the days rented at fair market value

**If Threshold Exceeded:**
The yacht is treated as a "residence" and rental expense deductions are limited to rental income (similar to Section 183 but different mechanism).

### The 14-Day Safe Harbor

**Augusta Rule (IRC 280A(g)):**
If yacht is rented for fewer than 15 days per year, rental income is not taxable, and no rental expenses are deductible.

**Not Useful for Charter Business:** This rule is for minimal rental activity, not ongoing charter operations.

### Fair Rental Days Calculation

**Proper Classification of Days:**
- **Charter Days:** Days vessel is rented to charter guests at fair market rate
- **Personal Use Days:** Owner, family, or friends use without paying fair market rate
- **Maintenance Days:** Not counted as charter or personal
- **Deadhead/Transit Days:** Moving vessel between charters; not personal use
- **Marketing/Sea Trials:** Business use if genuine (potential buyers, familiarization for brokers)

**Personal Use Includes:**
- Owner and immediate family
- Any other person with an ownership interest
- Anyone using at less than fair market rental price
- Anyone in a reciprocal arrangement

**NOT Personal Use:**
- Days rented at fair market value to unrelated parties
- Days used for repairs and maintenance
- Days vessel is available for rent but not rented (if actively marketed)

### Expense Deduction Limitation if 280A Applies

If personal use exceeds 14 days or 10% threshold:

**Deduction Ceiling:** Business deductions limited to:
Gross rental income
MINUS mortgage interest and property taxes allocable to rental use
MINUS other rental expenses (operating costs)
= LIMIT on depreciation and other deductions

**This creates a problem:** May not be able to deduct all depreciation.

**Strategy to Avoid 280A Limits:**
1. **Keep personal use ≤ 14 days AND ≤ 10% of charter days**
2. **Charge owner fair market rental value for all personal use** (then it's not "personal use" for 280A purposes)

### Fair Market Value Requirement

**Critical Compliance Point:**
Any time owner uses vessel, must charge and actually collect fair market rental value.

**Determining Fair Market Value:**
- Comparable charter rates for similar vessels in same market
- Published rate cards from charter management company
- Adjusted for season (high/low)
- Same rates charged to unrelated charter clients
- Include all standard charter terms (APA, crew gratuity, etc.)

**Documentation:**
- Written charter agreement between owner and LLC
- Invoice at market rates
- Payment from owner personal funds to LLC
- Treat exactly like arm's-length charter

**Example for 7-Day Owner Charter:**
- Market Rate: $80,000/week base + 30% APA ($24,000)
- Total Charter Cost: $104,000
- Plus crew gratuity (15-20%): $16,000-$20,000
- **Owner Pays LLC: $120,000-$124,000**

This seems expensive, but:
- Owner "pays" LLC (both controlled by owner, so money flows in circle)
- Creates charter income for LLC
- Avoids personal use classification
- Preserves business deduction treatment

## Passive Activity Loss Rules (Section 469)

### Passive Activity Classification

**Default Rule for Rental Activities:**
IRC Section 469 generally treats all rental activities as "passive" regardless of participation.

**Passive Loss Limitation:**
Passive losses can only offset passive income, not active income (wages, business income) or portfolio income (interest, dividends).

**Problem for High-Income Yacht Owner:**
- If charter yacht generates loss (common in early years due to depreciation)
- And yacht activity is passive
- Then losses cannot offset owner's active income from Cohesity

### Exceptions Allowing Current Loss Deduction

#### Exception 1: Material Participation

**Generally, rental activities are passive regardless of participation.** However, IRS regulations provide exceptions.

**Exception for Significant Personal Services:**
If significant personal services are provided in connection with making the property available for customer use, the activity is NOT automatically passive—regular trade or business rules apply (Temp. Reg. 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)).

**Charter Yachts MAY Qualify:**
Crewed yacht charters involve substantial services:
- Professional crew operating vessel
- Navigation and seamanship
- Meal preparation
- Hospitality services
- Entertainment
- Water sports instruction

**IRS Position:**
Rev. Proc. 89-12 provides safe harbor for rental activities to be treated as non-passive if certain conditions met. Yacht charters with crew may qualify.

**Material Participation Test (If Activity Not Per Se Passive):**
Must meet one of seven tests (IRC 469(h)):

**Most Relevant Tests:**
1. **500-Hour Test:** Participate more than 500 hours during year
2. **Substantially All Test:** Participation constitutes substantially all participation by all individuals
3. **100-Hour Test:** Participate more than 100 hours AND no one else participates more

**Application for Charter Yacht:**
- Owner participates in management: Reviewing bookings, financial reports, meeting with charter manager, vessel inspections
- Charter manager and crew do operational work
- Likely can meet 100-hour test if owner actively manages

**Material Participation Strategy:**
- Document 100+ hours annually in management activities
- Detailed time logs
- Business meetings, financial reviews, strategic planning
- Vessel inspections and oversight
- Marketing and business development

**Important:** Material participation alone doesn't override passive rental categorization—need the "significant services" exception to apply first.

#### Exception 2: Real Estate Professional

**Not Applicable:** Real estate professional exception under IRC 469(c)(7) applies only to rental real estate, not personal property like yachts.

#### Exception 3: $25,000 Active Participation Allowance

**Not Applicable:** The $25,000 rental real estate loss allowance for moderate-income taxpayers phases out at AGI above $100,000, irrelevant for high-income owner.

### Passive Activity Strategy for Charter Yacht

**Optimal Approach:**
1. **Argue Significant Services Exception:** Crewed charter yacht with full hospitality services is not a passive rental
2. **Material Participation:** Document 100+ hours of management activities annually
3. **Result:** Activity is non-passive, losses can offset active income

**Conservative Approach:**
Treat as passive, meaning:
- Losses carried forward until passive income or disposition
- Provides long-term tax deferral
- Losses ultimately deductible when vessel sold

**Practical Reality:**
- Year 1-2: Likely losses due to depreciation
- Year 3+: Should be profitable as charter base established
- Even if losses suspended, significant first-year deductions (179, bonus) provide immediate tax benefits
- Suspended losses create tax basis, reducing gain on eventual sale

**Importance for Section 199A:**
Section 199A QBI deduction requires material participation for certain categories—see below.

## Section 199A: Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction

### Overview of Section 199A

**The 23% Pass-Through Deduction:**
As of 2026, IRC Section 199A allows owners of pass-through businesses (LLCs, S-Corps, partnerships) to deduct 23% of Qualified Business Income (permanent increase from 20% under One Big Beautiful Bill Act 2025).

**Potential Tax Benefit:**
- Charter Net Income: $300,000
- Section 199A Deduction: $69,000 (23% of $300,000)
- **Tax Savings:** $25,875 at 37% tax bracket

### QBI Deduction Requirements

#### 1. Qualified Trade or Business

**Generally Qualifies:** Charter yacht operation conducted through pass-through entity (LLC)

**Exclusions:**
- Specified Service Trade or Business (SSTB): Charter yacht is NOT an SSTB
- Services as an employee: Not applicable

#### 2. Qualified Business Income (QBI)

**QBI Includes:**
- Charter revenue (gross charter income)
- Minus deductible expenses
- Includes depreciation deductions
- = Net business income

**QBI Excludes:**
- Investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains)
- Wage income
- Guaranteed payments to partners (if partnership structure)

#### 3. Taxable Income Threshold (2026)

**Phase-In Ranges (Adjusted for Inflation under 2025 OBBBA):**
- Single: Begins at $230,000, ends at $305,000
- Married Filing Jointly: Begins at $460,000, ends at $610,000

**Above Threshold:**
Deduction becomes subject to wage/property basis limitations.

#### 4. Wage and Property Basis Limitation

**For Taxpayers Above Threshold:**
QBI deduction limited to greater of:
- 50% of W-2 wages paid by business, OR
- 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of unadjusted basis of qualified property

**Application to Charter Yacht:**

**W-2 Wages:**
- Crew wages reported on W-2: $150,000-$400,000 depending on crew size
- 50% of $300,000 wages = $150,000 wage limit

**Qualified Property:**
- Unadjusted basis of yacht: $7,000,000
- 2.5% of $7,000,000 = $175,000
- Plus 25% of W-2 wages ($75,000)
- = $250,000 combined limit

**Deduction Calculation (If Above Threshold):**
- 23% of QBI: $69,000 (if QBI = $300,000)
- Wage/Property Limit: $250,000
- **Allowed Deduction: $69,000** (lesser of QBI limit and W/P limit)

**Key Insight:** High-value yacht ($7M) with crew provides ample wage and property basis to support full Section 199A deduction even for high-income owners.

#### 5. Material Participation Requirement

**New Under 2025 OBBBA:**
The new $400 minimum deduction requires material participation. While the main QBI deduction doesn't have a blanket material participation requirement, passive activities may have limitations.

**Best Practice:**
Document material participation (100+ hours, regular management) to ensure full Section 199A benefits and avoid potential challenges.

### Section 199A Strategy for Charter Yacht

**Maximize W-2 Wages:**
- Employ crew as W-2 employees (not independent contractors)
- Include all crew: captain, deckhands, chef, stewardess, engineer
- Properly document payroll and file quarterly 941s

**Maximize Qualified Property:**
- Depreciable yacht asset provides large property basis
- All qualifying improvements add to basis
- Financing doesn't reduce unadjusted basis

**Material Participation:**
- Track management hours meticulously
- Meet 100-hour test minimum
- Document all business activities

**Project Section 199A Benefit:**
Assuming $300,000 average net charter income (Years 3-7):
- Annual 199A deduction: $69,000
- Tax savings at 37% bracket: $25,530/year
- **5-Year cumulative savings: $127,650**

## Depreciation: Section 179, Bonus Depreciation, MACRS

### Asset Classification

**Yachts are classified as "listed property" under IRC Section 280F and depreciated as 10-year property under MACRS.**

**Recovery Period:** 10 years
**Depreciation Method:** 200% declining balance (double-declining balance)
**Convention:** Half-year (first and last year)
**Applicable MACRS Class:** GDS 10-year property

### Section 179 Expensing Election

**2026 Limits:**
- **Maximum Deduction:** $1,250,000 (indexed for inflation)
- **Phase-Out Threshold:** Equipment purchases exceeding $3,100,000 begin phase-out
- **Dollar-for-Dollar Reduction:** Over threshold, deduction reduced by excess

**Application to $7M Yacht:**
- Yacht cost $7,000,000 exceeds phase-out threshold
- $7,000,000 - $3,100,000 = $3,900,000 excess
- $1,250,000 - $3,900,000 = **$0 Section 179 deduction available**

**Result:** Section 179 NOT available for $7M yacht due to phase-out.

**Alternative:** If yacht cost less than $3.1M, full Section 179 would be available (up to taxable income limit).

**Partial Solutions:**
- Allocate certain equipment separately: Tender, jet skis, dive equipment, electronic equipment
- These might qualify individually under different rules
- Practically limited benefit

### Bonus Depreciation

**2026 Rate:** 20% (phase-down schedule: 60% in 2024, 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026, 0% in 2027)

**Bonus Depreciation Allows:**
Immediate first-year deduction of qualifying percentage of asset cost.

**Requirements:**
1. New or used property (used property qualified under TCJA through 2026)
2. Original use by taxpayer OR acquired used and no prior related-party use
3. Placed in service during tax year

**Application to $7M Yacht (2026):**
- Yacht cost: $7,000,000
- Section 179: $0 (phased out)
- **Bonus Depreciation (20%): $1,400,000**
- Remaining Basis: $5,600,000

**Business Use Percentage:**
If yacht has any personal use, must reduce by personal use percentage.

**Example with 5% Personal Use:**
- Business use: 95%
- Depreciable basis: $7,000,000 × 95% = $6,650,000
- Bonus depreciation: $6,650,000 × 20% = $1,330,000

**Critical 2026 Consideration:**
- Bonus depreciation is 20% in 2026
- Drops to 0% in 2027
- **Time-Sensitive:** Closing purchase in 2026 provides $1.4M additional first-year deduction vs. 2027

**After 2026:**
Unless Congress extends bonus depreciation, no bonus will be available for purchases after 2026.

- Purchase Price: $7,000,000

### Depreciation Strategy Considerations

#### Maximize First-Year Deductions

**Advantage:**
- Immediate tax benefit
- Higher present value of tax savings
- Cash flow benefit

**When Appropriate:**
- High current-year income
- Expect income to decrease in future years
- Want maximum upfront tax reduction

#### Consider Lighter First-Year Depreciation

**Advantage:**
- Shows profit sooner (helps Section 183 profit test)
- Smooths income over time
- Useful if anticipating higher future tax rates

**Methods:**
- Elect out of bonus depreciation (Form 4562, line 19)
- Elect straight-line MACRS instead of 200% DB
- Elect ADS (Alternative Depreciation System) for longer recovery (not recommended)

**When Appropriate:**
- Concerned about Section 183 hobby loss challenge
- Want to show profit in Years 2-3
- Already have sufficient deductions from other sources
- Anticipate tax rate increases

### Depreciation Recapture on Sale

**Ordinary Income Recapture:**
Under IRC Section 1245, depreciation previously deducted is recaptured as ordinary income upon sale.

**Example:**
- Purchase Price: $7,000,000
- Total Depreciation Claimed: $6,650,000 (over 11 years)
- Adjusted Basis: $350,000
- Sale Price: $3,000,000 (after depreciation/market decline)
- Gain: $3,000,000 - $350,000 = $2,650,000
- **Recapture (Ordinary Income): $2,650,000** (limited to depreciation taken)

**Tax on Recapture:**
- $2,650,000 × 37% = $980,500

**Planning Considerations:**
- Depreciation deductions save tax at ordinary rates now
- Recapture is taxed at ordinary rates later
- Time value of money favors current deductions
- Consider tax rate changes in future years

**Installment Sale Option:**
If sold on installment (owner financing), gain can be spread over multiple years.

# 5. BUSINESS VS PERSONAL USE GUIDELINES

## The Personal Use Problem

The most scrutinized aspect of yacht charter taxation is personal use. The IRS understands that yacht ownership involves lifestyle and personal enjoyment, creating inherent skepticism about genuine business purpose. Proper segregation of business and personal use is absolutely critical for tax compliance and audit defense.

## Personal Use Definitions and Thresholds

### What Constitutes "Personal Use"

**IRC Section 280A Personal Use Days Include:**

1. **Owner and Family:** Any day the owner or family members use the yacht
2. **Related Parties:** Co-owners, partners, members of LLC
3. **Below Fair Market Rental:** Anyone using at less than fair market value
4. **Reciprocal Arrangements:** Someone else's property in exchange for yacht use
5. **Non-Arms Length:** Friends, business associates not paying fair market rate

**NOT Personal Use:**

1. **Fair Market Rental:** Day rented to unrelated party at full fair market value
2. **Maintenance Days:** Days vessel unavailable due to maintenance, repairs
3. **Positioning Days:** Moving vessel between charter locations (deadhead)
4. **Charter Prep:** Preparing vessel between charters
5. **Marketing Events:** Legitimate broker shows, sea trials with prospective charter clients
6. **Crew Evaluation:** Captain taking vessel out for crew training (must be documented)

### Critical Personal Use Thresholds

#### 14-Day Safe Harbor

**IRC Section 280A(d):**
If personal use does NOT exceed the greater of:
- 14 days, OR
- 10% of total days rented at fair market value

Then vessel is NOT treated as a "dwelling unit used as a residence" and rental activity receives favorable business treatment.

**Example 1: Stay Under 14 Days**
- Total charter days: 120
- Personal use: 12 days
- 10% threshold: 12 days
- Personal use (12) ≤ both 14 days and 12 days ✓
- **Result:** Full business deductions allowed

**Example 2: Exceed Threshold**
- Total charter days: 100
- Personal use: 20 days
- 10% threshold: 10 days
- Personal use (20) > both 14 days and 10 days ✗
- **Result:** Dwelling unit rental limitations apply, deductions limited to income

#### Why 14 Days Matters

**Below 14 Days:**
- All depreciation deductible
- Losses can offset other income (if not passive activity)
- Full business expense deductions
- No allocation required (if properly documented as business)

**Above 14 Days:**
- Expense deductions limited to gross income from charter activity
- Depreciation deductions limited after other expenses
- Cannot use charter losses to offset other income
- Defeats primary tax benefit

### Personal Use Tracking System

**Daily Classification Required:**

Maintain a detailed calendar classifying every single day:

| Day Type | Definition | Counts As |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| **Charter Day** | Paying charter guests aboard | Business |
| **Personal Use** | Owner/family aboard not at FMV | Personal |
| **Paid Owner Use** | Owner aboard, paid FMV to LLC | Business (if legitimate) |
| **Maintenance** | Repairs, haul-out, unavailable | Neither |
| **Positioning** | Deadhead between locations | Business (if legitimate) |
| **Charter Prep** | Cleaning, provisioning between charters | Business |
| **Available/Listed** | No charter, but actively marketed | Neither |
| **Crew Training** | Legitimate training operations | Business (if documented) |
| **Broker Events** | Familiarization trips for brokers | Business (if documented) |
| **Sea Trials** | Prospective charter clients | Business |

**Documentation for Each Day:**
- Date
- Classification
- If charter: Charter agreement, payment record
- If personal: Note owner aboard
- If maintenance: Invoice, work order
- If positioning: Reason, destination, business purpose
- If training/marketing: Detailed description, participants, purpose

**Software Solutions:**
- Custom Excel/Google Sheets template
- Yacht management software (YachtCloud, Sailogy)
- Simple calendar with detailed notes

**Example Daily Log Entry:**
```

```

## Fair Market Value Requirement

### Establishing Fair Market Value

**IRS Requirement:**
Any personal use by owner or related parties must be charged at "fair market value" to avoid being classified as personal use.

**Fair Market Value Defined:**
The price that would be paid in an arms-length transaction between unrelated parties for comparable charter service.

**Methods to Determine FMV:**

#### 1. Published Rate Card
Charter management company's published rates for your vessel.

**Example Rate Card (80-foot yacht):**
- High Season (Dec-Apr): $95,000/week + 30% APA
- Shoulder Season (May, Nov): $80,000/week + 30% APA
- Low Season (Jun-Oct): $70,000/week + 30% APA

**Plus Mandatory Expenses:**
- APA (Advance Provisioning Allowance): 30% of charter rate (fuel, food, beverages, dockage)
- Crew Gratuity: 15-20% of charter rate (customary, typically paid)

**Total Owner Cost for 1-Week High Season Personal Use:**
- Base Rate: $95,000
- APA (30%): $28,500
- Crew Gratuity (17.5%): $16,625
- **Total: $140,125**

Owner must pay LLC $140,125 for the week.

#### 2. Comparable Charter Rates
Research rates for comparable vessels in same market.

**Sources:**
- CharterWorld.com
- YachtCharterFleet.com
- Camper & Nicholsons, Burgess, Fraser listings
- Direct charter management company data

**Comparability Factors:**
- Length (±10 feet)
- Year and condition
- Builder/model (similar quality)
- Location and season
- Amenities and toys
- Crew size and service level

**Example Comparable Search:**
For 80-foot Azimut, search:
- 75-85 foot motor yachts
- Azimut, Sunseeker, Princess, Ferretti brands
- Caribbean or Florida
- Same season
- Average rates: $80,000-$110,000/week

Use median or low-mid range for defensibility.

#### 3. Independent Appraisal
Engage yacht broker to provide written opinion of fair market charter rate.

**When Useful:**
- Unique vessel with few comparables
- Anticipating significant owner use
- Building audit defense
- High-value yacht

**Cost:** $1,000-$3,000

**Benefit:** Professional third-party determination of FMV.

### Charging and Collecting FMV

**Critical Compliance Steps:**

#### 1. Written Charter Agreement
Execute formal charter agreement between owner personally and LLC.

**Agreement Must Include:**
- Parties: Owner (individual) as charterer, LLC as owner
- Vessel description
- Charter dates and duration
- Charter rate (at fair market value)
- Payment terms
- APA amount and purpose
- Standard charter terms (cancellation, liability waiver, etc.)
- Signatures and date

**Use standard CYBA or MYBA charter agreement form** (same form used for unrelated charters).

#### 2. Invoice from LLC to Owner
LLC issues invoice to owner for charter amount.

**Invoice Details:**
- Date
- Invoice number (sequential with other charters)
- Description: "7-Day Yacht Charter Services, [dates]"
- Charter rate: $95,000
- APA (30%): $28,500
- Total due: $123,500
- Payment terms: Due before charter commencement

#### 3. Payment from Owner to LLC
Owner makes payment from personal account to LLC business account.

**Payment Methods:**
- Wire transfer (best documentation)
- Check from personal account
- ACH transfer

**Must Be Real Transaction:**
- Actual money movement
- From owner's personal funds to LLC account
- Properly recorded in both owner's and LLC's records

**Circular Money Flow:**
- Owner pays LLC (outflow from personal)
- LLC receives income (charter revenue)
- LLC pays expenses and taxes
- LLC may distribute net income to owner (as member distribution)

**Net Effect:**
- Owner paid $123,500 for charter
- LLC reports $123,500 charter income (taxable)
- LLC deducts depreciation, expenses against that income
- Due to depreciation, LLC likely has net loss despite charter income
- LLC loss offsets owner's other income (if non-passive)

**Tax Arbitrage:**
- Owner's payment is not deductible (personal expense)
- But LLC's loss from depreciation IS deductible
- Net: Owner gets indirect tax benefit

#### 4. Charter Proceeds Used for Business
LLC must use charter proceeds (including owner's payment) for legitimate business purposes: expenses, debt service, capital improvements.

**Cannot:**
- Pay owner proceeds back immediately as disguised return of payment
- Use proceeds for owner's personal expenses
- Treat differently than other charter revenues

**Can:**
- Apply to operating expenses (crew, fuel, dockage)
- Pay down yacht financing
- Reinvest in vessel improvements
- Retain as working capital
- Distribute to members as normal LLC distribution (reported as income)

### Personal Use Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Zero Personal Use
**Concept:** Owner never uses yacht personally; only charters to unrelated parties.

**Advantages:**
- Maximum tax benefits
- No personal use allocation
- Strongest audit defense
- Clearest business purpose

**Disadvantages:**
- Owner doesn't enjoy yacht
- Defeats part of ownership purpose

**Appropriate For:**
- Pure investment mindset
- Owner has other boats for personal use
- Primary goal is tax benefits and ROI

#### Strategy 2: Minimal Personal Use (<14 Days)
**Concept:** Owner uses yacht sparingly, staying well under 14-day threshold.

**Implementation:**
- Budget 10 personal days per year
- Charge self fair market value for those days
- Pay LLC from personal funds
- Document meticulously

**Advantages:**
- Owner gets some enjoyment
- Stays under safe harbor
- Full business deductions maintained
- Legitimate business characterization

**Disadvantages:**
- Very limited personal use
- Must pay significant charter rates for own use
- High cost for enjoyment

**Appropriate For:**
- Owner's primary goal is tax optimization
- Owner's personal use is primarily other locations/boats
- Owner willing to pay market rates for occasional use

#### Strategy 3: Fair Market Value for All Personal Use
**Concept:** Owner can use yacht as much as desired if pays fair market value every time.

**Implementation:**
- No limit on personal days
- Every personal day: written charter agreement, invoice, payment at FMV
- All personal use treated as charter revenue

**Advantages:**
- Unlimited owner usage
- Maintains business characterization (all use is "chartered")
- Avoids personal use classification

**Disadvantages:**
- Expensive: Must pay $10,000-$20,000+ per day of personal use
- Cash flow: Constantly paying LLC
- Circular transaction (paying entity you control)

**Tax Analysis:**
- Owner pays LLC: Not tax-deductible (personal expense)
- LLC receives income: Taxable charter revenue
- LLC deducts expenses: Depreciation and operating costs
- Net LLC income/loss: Reported on owner's return

**Net Effect:**
- If LLC has loss (due to depreciation): Owner's payment generates taxable income, but LLC loss offsets owner's other income—favorable.
- If LLC is profitable: Owner pays tax on LLC profit, and paid charter rate from after-tax dollars—less favorable.

**Appropriate For:**
- High-net-worth owners who want significant personal use
- Owners with substantial other income to offset
- Situations where yacht is chartered heavily (150+ days/year), so owner use is minor percentage

#### Strategy 4: Separate Personal and Charter Yachts
**Concept:** Own two vessels: one personal, one pure charter business.

**Implementation:**
- Personal yacht: Smaller, personal ownership (individual or revocable trust)
- Charter yacht: $7M vessel in LLC, pure business use

**Advantages:**
- Clean separation
- Charter yacht has zero personal use issues
- Personal yacht used freely without tax complications

**Disadvantages:**
- Capital intensive: Two vessels
- Double operating costs
- Double crews, insurance, maintenance

**Appropriate For:**
- Ultra-high-net-worth individuals
- Those who want frequent personal yacht use AND tax-optimized charter business
- Family offices managing multiple assets

### Red Flags to Avoid

**IRS Audit Triggers:**
1. **Zero or Minimal Charter Revenue:** If yacht sits unused or rarely chartered, suggests hobby not business
2. **Consistent Losses:** Years of losses without profitability raises Section 183 concerns
3. **High Personal Use:** Owner uses yacht extensively
4. **Below-Market Pricing:** Owner charged less than comparable charters
5. **Related-Party Charters:** Primarily chartered to friends, family, business associates at preferential rates
6. **Lack of Marketing:** No website, no charter platform listings, no professional marketing
7. **Informal Operations:** No written agreements, poor record-keeping, commingled funds
8. **Luxury Lifestyle Audit:** IRS lifestyle audits of high-income taxpayers often examine yacht ownership

**Mitigation:**
- Aggressive charter marketing from Day 1
- Third-party professional charter management
- Fair market value pricing enforced consistently
- Detailed documentation of business purpose and operations
- Realistic profitability projections showing path to profit
- Professional business structure and recordkeeping

### Charter Management Fee Structures

**Typical Fee Models:**

#### Model 1: Percentage of Gross Revenue

- Owner pays: All vessel operating expenses

**Example:**

- Owner net revenue: $750,000
- Less operating expenses: ($650,000)
- Owner net income: $100,000

#### Model 2: Fixed Fee Plus Percentage
**Structure:**
- Fixed monthly management fee: $5,000-$10,000
- Plus booking commission: 10-15% per charter
- Owner retains higher percentage of revenue

**Example:**
- Fixed fee: $7,000/month × 12 = $84,000
- Gross charter revenue: $1,000,000
- Booking commission (12%): $120,000
- Total management cost: $204,000
- Owner net revenue: $796,000

**Advantage:** Predictable base cost, potentially lower total fees if charter revenue high.

#### Model 3: All-Inclusive Management
**Structure:**
- Single percentage (30-40%) covers ALL expenses
- Management company pays crew, insurance, maintenance, dockage
- Owner receives net proceeds only

**Example:**
- Gross charter revenue: $1,000,000
- Management fee (35%): $350,000
- All expenses paid by management company: $650,000
- Owner net proceeds: $0 (break-even) to $50,000

**Advantage:** Simplified, no expense management by owner, predictable.
**Disadvantage:** Less control, typically lower owner net, less favorable tax position (fewer deductible expenses flowing through).

**Tax Consideration:** Model 1 or 2 is preferable for tax purposes—shows more gross revenue and deductible expenses on owner's tax return, supporting business characterization.

2. **Request for Proposal:** Send yacht details, request fee quote and services description
3. **Interviews:** Video or in-person meetings with 3-4 finalists
4. **Reference Checks:** Speak with 2-3 current clients of each finalist
5. **Contract Review:** Attorney review of management agreement
6. **Final Selection:** Choose based on comprehensive evaluation

**Timeline:** 4-8 weeks from initial research to signed agreement

## Charter Operations and Logistics

### Charter Booking Process

**Typical Charter Booking Flow:**

1. **Client Inquiry**
   - Potential client contacts charter manager or broker
   - Provides: Dates, destination, guest count, preferences
   - Requests: Rate quote, availability, vessel details

2. **Rate Quote and Proposal**
   - Charter manager prepares customized proposal
   - Includes: Charter rate, APA estimate, terms, photos
   - Sent to client via broker or direct

3. **Negotiations**
   - Dates, rate, inclusions
   - Special requests
   - Contract terms

4. **Charter Agreement Execution**
   - Standard CYBA or MYBA charter agreement prepared
   - Parties: Charterer (client) and LLC (yacht owner)
   - Signed by all parties

5. **Payment**
   - Deposit: 50% of charter fee due at signing (typical)
   - Balance and APA: Due 4-6 weeks before charter commencement
   - Wire transfer to charter management trust account or LLC

6. **Pre-Charter Planning**
   - Preference sheet completed by client (food, beverages, activities)
   - Crew provisions and prepares
   - Itinerary developed
   - Special requests arranged (flowers, gifts, off-boat excursions)

7. **Charter Execution**
   - Client arrives, welcomed aboard by crew
   - Crew provides full hospitality service
   - Captain manages navigation and itinerary
   - Chef prepares meals, stewardess provides service
   - Water sports, activities as requested

8. **APA Accounting**
   - Crew tracks expenses from APA (fuel, food, dockage, activities)
   - Daily or running accounting
   - End of charter: Final APA accounting provided
   - Surplus refunded or additional payment collected

9. **Crew Gratuity**
   - Customary: 15-20% of charter fee
   - Paid at end of charter (cash or wire)
   - Distributed among crew by captain

10. **Post-Charter**
    - Client feedback gathered
    - Yacht cleaned and prepared for next charter
    - Final financial accounting
    - Owner payment processed

# 7. INSURANCE & RISK MANAGEMENT

## Insurance Requirements for Charter Operations

Charter yacht operations require substantially different and more comprehensive insurance than private yacht ownership. Commercial charter coverage is mandatory for legitimate business operations and IRS compliance.

### Types of Insurance Coverage

#### 1. Hull & Machinery Insurance

**Coverage:**
Physical damage to the vessel itself from named perils: collision, grounding, fire, theft, sinking, weather, vandalism.

**Policy Structure:**
- Agreed Value: $7,000,000 (vessel purchase price)
- Deductible: $50,000-$100,000 (typical 1-2% of insured value)
- Geographic Limits: Defined cruising area (e.g., "Western Atlantic, Caribbean, Bahamas")
- Seasonal Restrictions: Hurricane season haul-out requirements or navigation limits
- Layup Periods: Reduced premium if vessel inactive

**Annual Premium:** $40,000-$70,000 (0.6-1.0% of insured value) for charter operations

**Higher Cost vs. Private Use:** Charter operations pay 30-50% higher premiums than private use due to increased exposure.

**Coverage Endorsements:**
- Charter Operations Endorsement: Required specifically for commercial charter
- Foreign Waters Extension: If leaving U.S. territorial waters
- Hurricane Coverage: May require deductible increase or haul-out provisions
- Named Skipper Clause: May limit who can operate vessel

#### 2. Protection & Indemnity (P&I) Insurance

**Coverage:**
Third-party liability for injury, death, and property damage caused by vessel operations.

**Typical Limits:** $5,000,000-$20,000,000 per occurrence

**What's Covered:**
- Bodily injury to charter guests
- Bodily injury to crew (Jones Act liability)
- Property damage to other vessels or property
- Medical expenses for injured parties
- Wreck removal and pollution cleanup
- Legal defense costs
- Passenger displacement and accommodation

**Annual Premium:** $20,000-$40,000 depending on limits

**Jones Act Coverage:**
U.S. maritime law (Jones Act, Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act) provides special protections for maritime workers:
- Crew members can sue for negligence
- Strict liability for unseaworthiness
- Medical expenses and lost wages
- Pain and suffering damages

**Charter Guest Liability:**
- Slip and fall injuries
- Water sports accidents
- Medical emergencies
- Evacuation costs
- Death or serious injury claims

**Pollution Liability:**
- Oil or fuel spills
- Clean-up costs
- Environmental damage claims
- Regulatory fines

#### 3. Crew Coverage

**Workers' Compensation:**
Not always required for maritime crew (Jones Act provides alternative), but recommended.

**Coverage Includes:**
- Medical expenses for work-related injuries
- Lost wages during recovery
- Disability benefits
- Death benefits

**Cost:** $10,000-$20,000/year depending on crew size and payroll

**Crew Medical Insurance:**
- Employer-sponsored health insurance
- Cost: $500-$1,000/month per crew member
- Often required to attract quality crew

#### 4. Commercial Umbrella Liability

**Coverage:**
Excess liability coverage above P&I limits.

**Typical Structure:**
- $10,000,000-$50,000,000 umbrella
- Over primary P&I policy
- Broader coverage than underlying policy

**Cost:** $5,000-$15,000/year

**When Necessary:**
- High-net-worth owner (target for lawsuits)
- Large yacht with many guests
- High-risk operations (offshore, adventure charters)

#### 5. Loss of Charter Revenue (Optional)

**Coverage:**
Reimburses lost charter income if vessel is damaged and unable to charter.

**Example:**
- Vessel damaged in storm
- Out of service for 3 months repairs
- Lost charter income: $300,000
- Insurance pays up to limit

**Cost:** $5,000-$15,000/year
**Limits:** $200,000-$1,000,000 per incident

**Consideration:** May not be cost-effective given premium vs. potential payout.

### U.S. Coast Guard Documentation

**Certificate of Documentation (COD):**
U.S. flagged vessels over 5 net tons can (and for charter, generally should) be documented with U.S. Coast Guard.

**Advantages of USCG Documentation:**
1. **Legal Recognition:** Provides clear vessel title and ownership
2. **Financing:** Required by most lenders for yacht financing
3. **International Operations:** Recognized globally, easier foreign port entry
4. **Insurance:** Often required by insurers
5. **Professional Appearance:** Indicates legitimate commercial operation

**Documentation Process:**

1. **Application:** Form CG-1258 (Application for Initial Issue, Exchange, or Replacement of Certificate of Documentation)
2. **Builder's Certificate:** Proof of vessel construction or previous documentation
3. **Bill of Sale:** Chain of title to current owner
4. **Evidence of Ownership:** LLC articles of organization, operating agreement
5. **Tonnage Measurement:** May require surveyor if not already measured
6. **Fees:** $100-$150 application fee

**Endorsements:**
- **Coastwise:** For transportation of passengers/cargo between U.S. ports (requires U.S. build and ownership)
- **Fishery:** Commercial fishing (not applicable)
- **Registry:** International trade (not typical for charter yachts)
- **Recreation:** Private use only (NOT for charter)

**For Charter Operations:**
Most common is no endorsement or recreation endorsement, depending on operation type and Coast Guard interpretation.

**Documentation Timeline:** 4-8 weeks from application to certificate issuance

**Annual Renewal:** Not required; valid until ownership change or vessel disposition. Must update if ownership or vessel details change.

**Vessel Marking:**
Documented vessels do NOT display state registration numbers. Must display:
- Vessel name on stern and both bows
- Hailing port on stern (typically "Fort Lauderdale, FL" or LLC jurisdiction)
- Official number (interior, not visible)

### Risk Management Best Practices

**Safety Management System (SMS):**

**Components:**
1. **Safety Manual:** Comprehensive vessel operations and safety procedures
2. **Emergency Procedures:** Fire, flooding, man overboard, medical emergency, grounding
3. **Crew Training Plan:** Regular drills and training documentation
4. **Maintenance Schedule:** Preventive maintenance log
5. **Pre-Departure Checklist:** Standard checklist before every voyage
6. **Guest Briefing:** Safety orientation for every charter party
7. **Incident Reporting:** Process for documenting any incidents or near-misses

**Implementation:**
- Charter management company may provide template SMS
- Customize for specific vessel
- Train crew on all procedures
- Document all drills and training
- Annual review and update

**Benefits:**
- Insurance premium reduction (5-15% possible)
- Improved safety record
- IRS audit defense (demonstrates business-like operations)
- Guest confidence
- Regulatory compliance

**Crew Training:**

**Required Certifications:**
- **STCW (Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping):** International maritime training standard
- **First Aid/CPR:** All crew
- **Fire Fighting:** All crew
- **USCG Licenses:** Captain (Master), Engineer (if required based on tonnage)

**Recommended Training:**
- Guest service and hospitality
- Food safety and sanitation
- Water sports instruction
- Emergency response (man overboard, medical)

**Annual Refresher Training:**
- Safety drills (monthly)
- Medical scenarios (quarterly)
- Fire drills (monthly)
- Man overboard drills (quarterly)

**Guest Safety:**

**Pre-Charter Briefing (Mandatory):**
- Emergency procedures and equipment locations
- Life jacket location and use
- Fire extinguisher locations
- Emergency exits
- Man overboard procedures
- Communication protocols
- Restricted areas
- Water sports safety
- Alcohol and substance policies

**Documentation:**
- Briefing checklist signed by all guests
- Keep on board for duration of charter
- Include in post-charter records

**Waivers and Releases:**

**Charter Agreement Liability Provisions:**
Standard CYBA and MYBA charter agreements include comprehensive liability waivers and releases.

**Key Provisions:**
- Guest assumes risk of maritime activities
- Release of owner/crew from certain liabilities
- Indemnification for guest-caused damage
- Limitation of remedies to charter fee refund

**Additional Waivers:**
- Water sports activity waivers (jet ski, diving, tow sports)
- Minor participation consent forms
- Medical information and emergency contact

**Enforcement:**
Waivers are not absolute protection (cannot waive gross negligence or intentional harm), but provide significant defense in litigation.

**Compliance and Regulatory:**

**USCG Inspections:**
- Commercial vessels carrying 6 or fewer passengers for hire are generally NOT subject to USCG Certificate of Inspection (COI)
- Vessels carrying 7+ passengers require full COI and inspected vessel status (much more onerous)

**Strategy:** Keep charter guest limit at 6 or fewer to avoid COI requirements.

**Environmental Compliance:**
- No discharge of oil or fuel
- Sewage discharge restrictions (3+ miles offshore or approved treatment system)
- Garbage disposal regulations (MARPOL Annex V)
- Gray water discharge (state regulations vary)

**Tax and Business Compliance:**
- Business licenses (state, local, port authorities)
- Sales tax collection (if applicable in jurisdiction)
- Crew payroll tax compliance
- Foreign cruising permits (customs and immigration)
- Fishing permits (if offering sport fishing)
